## On the Aesthetics of Circuit Design
**2024** · *Design, Hardware, Philosophy*

### The Physicality of Logic
There is a certain undeniable beauty in the trace routing of a Printed Circuit Board (PCB). Unlike software, where abstraction layers pile upon one another until the underlying machine is obscured, hardware design forces you to confront the physical reality of electricity.

When routing a differential pair for high-speed signals, you aren't just connecting point A to point B; you are managing impedance, minimizing crosstalk, and ensuring that the electrons arrive at their destination in perfect synchrony. The constraints of the physical world—geometry, material properties, thermodynamics—become the canvas upon which logic is painted.

In this sense, a well-designed circuit board is as much a work of art as it is a functional component. It represents a harmony between the abstract schematic and the tangible reality.

> "To design hardware is to negotiate with the laws of physics."

## Digital Gardening
**2023** · *Productivity, PKM*

### Growing Ideas
I've recently started treating my personal knowledge management not as a library, but as a garden. 

A library is static; you archive information to retrieve it later. A garden is alive; you plant seeds (fleeting thoughts), water them (connect them to other ideas), and occasionally prune them (refine or discard).

This shift in perspective has changed how I document my engineering projects. Instead of writing a final "report" at the end, I try to cultivate the documentation alongside the development process. The "Zen Land" section of this website is an extension of that garden—a place to share the blooms that are ready to be seen.

## The Algorithm of Serendipity
**2023** · *Algorithms, Life*

### Random Walks
In graph theory, a random walk is a mathematical object, known as a stochastic or random process, that describes a path that consists of a succession of random steps on some mathematical space.

It’s fascinating how applicable this is to finding inspiration. If we optimize our lives purely for efficiency (shortest path algorithms), we often miss the local maxima that exist just off the beaten path. Allowing a degree of randomness—reading a paper from a different field, attending a talk you don't understand, talking to a stranger—increases the probability of serendipitous discovery.

Optimization is efficient, but exploration is necessary for evolution.
